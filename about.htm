About the Visualization

CS 498 - Summer 2017 - Final Project - Cynthia Johnson - cjj4

This visualization is an Interactive Slide Show. The question or lesson that this visualization attempts to explore is whether bank failures throughout the past 17 years were random events or in a pattern of area of the country.

Data from the Federal Depositors Insurance Corporation (FDIC) The list of all banking institutions was retrieved and then limited to the failed institutions.

For each bank, the asset value is defined as the assets owned by the institution. For failed institutions, this value represents the asset value at the time of failure.

Slide 1 is the overall context of all bank failures from 2000 to 2017. The default display is a graph showing failed asset totals in dollars by FDIC Region. The charts show that a total of $709 Billion (B) in assets has failed. In 2008, more than half, $371B, failed. And in 2008, the San Francisco region accounted for $360B.

Buttons at the bottom allow alternate graphs to view asset failures by state and by year. The Next button advances to Page 2.

Because the San Francisco region accounts for the largest total asset failure by a significant margin, it is the subject of the 2nd slide. The default view is failed assets by state. Buttons at the bottom allow alternate graphs to view asset failures by state and by year. The Next button advances to Page 3.

Because Nevada in 2008 accounts for a significantly large share of asset failures, it is the subject of the 3rd slide. The default view is failed assets by bank name. A deeper look reveals that the failure of Washington Mutual Bank accounted for $307B. Buttons at the bottom allow alternate graphs to view asset failures by city or bank name.

Project Rubric Notes:
1. Hybrid structure: Interactive Slide Show. Slides are author-driven with 3 in sequence. Reader may select different views to look at data presented in each slide.
2. Utilization of scenes: Slide 1 provides the full scene of all bank failures over 17 years. Each successive slide provides a description of the data available to view.
3. Annotations: Each bar in the graphs includes a tool tip with the actual value of the bar.
4. Parameters: Each button sends a different parameter to the graph drawing allowing the viewer to choose different dimensions of data segmentation.
5. Triggers: Two different triggers are used. Buttons to choose a different dimensional view of the data as well as buttons to move to other slides.
